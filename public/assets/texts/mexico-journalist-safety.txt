Silenced: The War on Mexico's Press

By Roberto Vega
CULIACAN, Sinaloa, Mexico --

The newsroom of El Debate, one of the oldest daily newspapers in the state of Sinaloa, is quieter than it should be. Two desks near the back wall are empty. Their occupants, a crime reporter and an investigative editor, left the paper within the same month last year. One relocated to Mexico City after receiving a threatening phone call at his home. The other left journalism entirely and now works for a construction company. Neither has been replaced.

This is what a free press looks like when it is dying by degrees. Not through a single dramatic act of censorship but through an accumulation of fear, violence, and institutional failure that steadily empties the rooms where journalism happens.

Mexico is the deadliest country in the world for journalists outside of active war zones. According to the Committee to Protect Journalists, at least sixty reporters and media workers have been killed in Mexico since 2018, with the majority of cases linked to their coverage of organized crime, corruption, or the intersection of the two. The Article 19 organization, which monitors press freedom in Mexico, documented more than seven hundred attacks against journalists in the country during the most recent reporting year, including physical assaults, threats, digital harassment, and the theft or destruction of equipment.

The violence is concentrated in states where drug cartels exercise significant territorial control, including Sinaloa, Guerrero, Tamaulipas, Veracruz, and Michoacan, but it is not confined to those regions. Journalists covering local politics, land disputes, and environmental issues have also been targeted, often by actors with connections to both organized crime and local government. The line between the two in many Mexican municipalities is effectively nonexistent.

Impunity is the engine that drives the cycle. Ninety percent of crimes against journalists in Mexico are never solved, according to data compiled by the federal Special Prosecutor's Office for Attention to Crimes Against Freedom of Expression, known as FEADLE. The office, which was created specifically to address violence against the press, has secured convictions in fewer than two dozen cases since its establishment. Critics describe it as understaffed, underfunded, and operationally paralyzed by the same corruption that afflicts the broader Mexican justice system.

The federal government operates a protection mechanism for journalists and human rights defenders, established in 2012 under international pressure following a surge in killings. The mechanism provides panic buttons, bodyguards, armored vehicles, and in extreme cases relocation assistance to journalists facing verified threats. More than sixteen hundred journalists and activists are currently enrolled in the program. Several journalists who were killed had active protection measures at the time of their deaths.

Ana Lucia Torres, a journalist based in Culiacan who covers drug trafficking and has been enrolled in the protection mechanism for three years, describes the program as a bureaucratic exercise that provides the appearance of safety without the substance.

"They gave me a panic button," Torres says. "I pressed it once when a car followed me home from the office. The response took forty minutes. In forty minutes in this city, you can be killed, buried, and forgotten."

The effects on journalism are measurable and devastating. Self-censorship has become the dominant survival strategy for reporters working in high-risk areas. Entire subjects have become unpublishable. In some states, newspapers have stopped covering organized crime altogether, running wire service copy in place of local reporting. The result is an information vacuum that serves the interests of the very actors whose activities should be scrutinized.

Citizen journalism and digital outlets have partially filled the gap, with anonymous social media accounts and independent news sites publishing information that traditional media will not touch. But these platforms are also vulnerable. Administrators of crime-reporting social media pages have been kidnapped and killed, and the anonymity that protects some digital journalists also undermines their credibility and makes their information difficult to verify.

International press freedom organizations have repeatedly called on the Mexican government to strengthen its protection mechanisms, reform its justice system to address impunity, and publicly acknowledge the severity of the crisis. The response has been uneven. Former President Andres Manuel Lopez Obrador frequently dismissed press freedom concerns and publicly attacked critical journalists, accusing them of serving opposition interests. His successor has pledged a more constructive approach but has not yet implemented structural reforms.

In the newsroom of El Debate, editor Lucia Mendoza reviews the next day's front page. There is a story about a road construction project, a feature on a local school's anniversary, and a brief report on a shooting that killed two men in a neighborhood controlled by the Sinaloa Cartel. The shooting story is four paragraphs long and contains no analysis, no context, and no attribution beyond a police spokesperson.

Mendoza does not apologize for the brevity. She explains it.

"A longer story asks questions," she says. "Questions have consequences. I have reporters with families. I will not ask them to be brave. I will ask them to be alive tomorrow."

The empty desks at the back of the room say the rest.
