Unfinished Peace: Colombia's Long Road From Accords to Reality

By Diego Herrera
VISTA HERMOSA, Meta, Colombia --

The former guerrilla fighter who now goes by the name Carlos tends a cacao nursery on a hillside outside Vista Hermosa, a town in the eastern Colombian department of Meta that spent decades as a stronghold of the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia. He is forty-six years old. He joined the FARC at fifteen, fought for twenty-two years, and laid down his weapons in 2017 following the peace agreement signed between the guerrilla organization and the Colombian government. He has not held a rifle in seven years. He has, by his own count, planted more than four thousand cacao seedlings.

"People ask me if I am at peace," Carlos says, pruning a young tree with a machete that looks identical to the ones he once carried through the jungle for different purposes. "I am alive and I am working. In this country, for someone like me, that is peace enough."

The 2016 peace agreement between the Colombian government and the FARC was one of the most ambitious conflict resolution efforts in modern history. It ended more than five decades of armed struggle that killed over two hundred and sixty thousand people, displaced nearly eight million, and left deep scars across the country's social and political fabric. The accords included provisions for FARC combatants to disarm and reintegrate into civilian life, for rural development programs to address the root causes of the conflict, for a transitional justice system to hold perpetrators accountable while facilitating reconciliation, and for the substitution of illicit coca crops with legal alternatives.

Eight years later, the implementation record is mixed in ways that defy simple characterization. The FARC as a military organization has effectively ceased to exist. More than thirteen thousand former combatants formally entered the reintegration process, and the majority have remained in civilian life. Productive projects like Carlos's cacao farm are operating in several regions, supported by a combination of government funding and international aid. The transitional justice court, known as the JEP, has conducted hearings that have produced some of the most detailed public accounting of wartime atrocities in Colombian history, including testimony from former FARC commanders acknowledging kidnappings and killings.

But the peace that the accords promised to Colombia's most affected communities remains incomplete in critical ways. Rural development programs have been chronically underfunded. The government committed to building roads, schools, and health clinics in conflict-affected zones, but budget allocations have consistently fallen short of the targets established in the agreement. In departments like Meta, Caqueta, and Putumayo, many communities still lack paved roads and reliable access to medical care.

The coca substitution program, which was intended to give farmers economic alternatives to growing the raw material for cocaine production, has reached only a fraction of the families that signed up. More than ninety thousand families enrolled in the program, agreeing to voluntarily eradicate their coca crops in exchange for financial support and technical assistance in transitioning to legal agriculture. According to the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, fewer than half have received the full package of support they were promised. In the interim, many have replanted coca or abandoned their land entirely.

Most troublingly, violence has not ended. It has changed shape. The power vacuum left by the FARC's demobilization has been filled by a constellation of armed groups, including FARC dissident factions that rejected the peace agreement, the National Liberation Army or ELN, and narcotrafficking organizations that have expanded into former FARC territory. Assassinations of community leaders, human rights defenders, and former combatants have continued at alarming rates. The Colombian human rights ombudsman's office documented more than one hundred and ninety killings of social leaders in the most recent annual reporting period.

In Vista Hermosa, the dynamics are visible on a local scale. Carlos's cacao cooperative includes twenty-three former FARC members who are building a small processing facility to add value to their crop before selling it. The project has received funding from the European Union and technical support from a Colombian agricultural university. It is, by the standards of the reintegration program, a success story.

But the cooperative operates under the shadow of armed groups that control territory less than thirty kilometers away. Two of its members have received death threats. One was forced to temporarily relocate his family after armed men visited his home at night. The local police presence is minimal, and the army's operations in the area are focused on counter-narcotics objectives that do not necessarily align with the security needs of former combatants trying to farm legally.

The Colombian government under President Gustavo Petro has pursued negotiations with the remaining armed groups, including the ELN and several FARC dissident factions, seeking what it calls "total peace." The strategy is ambitious and controversial. Critics argue that negotiating with multiple armed actors simultaneously dilutes the government's leverage and signals that violence is an effective path to the bargaining table. Supporters counter that a comprehensive approach is the only way to address the interconnected conflicts that continue to destabilize large parts of the country.

Carlos does not follow the negotiations closely. He follows the weather, the cacao prices, and the security situation on the road between his farm and the nearest market town. He has built something tangible from the wreckage of a war he fought and lost. Whether the country around him can do the same remains an open question.
