White Gold, Dry Earth: The Hidden Cost of Chile's Lithium Boom

By Camila Reyes
SAN PEDRO DE ATACAMA, Chile --

From the rim of the Salar de Atacama, the lithium evaporation ponds stretch to the horizon like enormous geometric paintings, rectangles of turquoise, jade, and pale yellow arranged across the white salt flat. They are strikingly beautiful from a distance. Up close, they smell of brine and industrial chemistry, and the ground beside them is crusted with a residue that crunches underfoot like old snow.

Chile holds the world's largest proven lithium reserves, concentrated almost entirely beneath this high-altitude desert in the country's north. The Salar de Atacama alone accounts for roughly a quarter of global lithium production, a share that is expanding as demand for electric vehicle batteries and grid-scale energy storage systems accelerates worldwide. The Chilean government has declared lithium a strategic resource, and international mining companies are competing for concession rights that could be worth tens of billions of dollars over the coming decades.

The extraction process is deceptively simple. Companies pump lithium-rich brine from underground aquifers to the surface and spread it across vast evaporation ponds where the desert sun does the work of concentrating the mineral. The concentrated solution is then processed into lithium carbonate or lithium hydroxide for export. It requires relatively little energy compared to hard-rock lithium mining in places like Australia. What it requires is water, and water is precisely what the Atacama does not have.

The Atacama Desert is one of the driest places on Earth. Some weather stations in the region have never recorded a single drop of rain. The indigenous Atacameno communities who have lived here for centuries rely on a fragile network of underground aquifers, surface springs, and snowmelt channels to sustain their agriculture, their livestock, and their daily lives. Those same water sources are now under pressure from an extraction industry that pumps millions of liters of brine daily from beneath the salt flat.

Hydrologists studying the Salar de Atacama have documented a measurable decline in the water table surrounding the lithium operations. A study published by researchers at the University of Chile found that brine extraction was reducing freshwater availability in adjacent aquifers, a finding that lithium companies have disputed but that independent reviewers have broadly supported. The practical effects are visible in communities near the salar, where springs that once flowed year-round have slowed to trickles and pasturelands that supported llama and alpaca herds have dried to dust.

Cristina Ramos, a community leader in the Atacameno village of Toconao, stands beside a channel that once carried snowmelt from the Andes into an irrigation network serving several dozen small farms. The channel is dry. She says it has been dry for most of the last three years.

"They tell us the lithium is for saving the planet," Ramos says. "But they are killing our land to do it. This is the paradox no one in Santiago or Washington wants to talk about."

The green energy paradox she describes is one of the central tensions in the global energy transition. Electric vehicles produce zero tailpipe emissions, but the supply chains that feed them involve extraction processes that generate their own environmental and social costs. Lithium mining in Chile consumes water in a desert. Cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo relies on child labor. Nickel processing in Indonesia destroys tropical forests. The clean energy economy, in other words, is not as clean as its marketing suggests.

The Chilean government under President Gabriel Boric has attempted to navigate this tension by proposing a national lithium strategy that would give the state a majority stake in future mining operations and impose stricter environmental requirements. The plan has drawn opposition from both sides. Industry groups argue it will deter foreign investment and slow production at a time when global demand is surging. Indigenous communities say it does not go far enough to protect their water rights and grant them meaningful consent over operations that affect their territory.

Legal battles are proliferating. Several Atacameno communities have filed complaints with Chilean environmental courts, arguing that existing mining concessions were granted without adequate consultation as required under both Chilean law and the International Labour Organization's Convention 169 on indigenous rights. Court proceedings are slow, and mining continues while cases are pending.

Meanwhile, the global market is indifferent to these disputes. Lithium prices have fluctuated dramatically in recent years, spiking during periods of high demand and falling during supply gluts, but the long-term trajectory is upward. Every major automaker has announced electrification targets that depend on a stable lithium supply. Chile, sitting atop the world's largest reserves, is positioned to profit enormously from the transition.

The question is who pays the cost. In Toconao, Cristina Ramos watches the empty irrigation channel and does not need to consult a hydrological study to know the answer.
