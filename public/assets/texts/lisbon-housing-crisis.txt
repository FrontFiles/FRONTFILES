Priced Out of Home: Lisbon's Housing Affordability Collapse

By Rafael Correia
Lisbon, Portugal -- March 2026

Ana Sousa is a schoolteacher in Lisbon. She has worked in the public education system for fourteen years. She earns roughly 1,400 euros per month after taxes. Her rent, for a one-bedroom apartment in the Arroios neighborhood, is 1,100 euros. The math does not work, and she knows it does not work, and she stays because the alternative -- moving thirty or forty kilometers outside the city, where rents are lower but commute costs and time consume the savings -- does not work either.

"I am not poor," she said, sitting in a cafe near her school. "I have a professional career. I have a degree. And I cannot afford to live in the city where I work. Something is broken."

Sousa's situation is not exceptional. It is the baseline experience for a growing majority of Lisbon residents. The Portuguese capital, once considered one of Western Europe's most affordable cities, has undergone a housing price transformation so rapid and so severe that it has fundamentally altered the city's social composition. Average rents in Lisbon increased by approximately 130 percent between 2015 and 2025. In the same period, average wages rose by roughly 25 percent. The gap is not a gap. It is a chasm.

The causes are multiple and well documented, though their relative weight is debated. Portugal's Golden Visa program, which granted residency permits to foreign nationals who purchased property above a certain value, attracted billions of euros in real estate investment between its launch in 2012 and its modification in 2023. The program was credited with revitalizing Lisbon's historic center but criticized for inflating prices beyond the reach of local buyers. The government ended the property-purchase pathway in 2023, but the structural effects on the market persist.

Short-term rental platforms, particularly Airbnb, transformed thousands of residential units into tourist accommodation. At its peak, Lisbon had more than 25,000 registered short-term rental properties -- an extraordinary number for a city of approximately 550,000 residents within its municipal boundaries. Entire neighborhoods in the historic center, including Alfama, Mouraria, and parts of Graca, saw their resident populations decline by thirty to fifty percent as landlords converted apartments to tourist use.

The Portuguese government introduced regulations on short-term rentals in 2023, including restrictions on new licenses and tax incentives for landlords who convert units back to long-term leases. The measures have had modest effect. Conversion rates have been lower than projected, in part because the economics still favor tourist rentals in high-demand areas.

Foreign investment in Lisbon real estate extends well beyond the Golden Visa program. The city's reputation as a tech hub, its favorable tax regime for non-habitual residents, and its quality of life have attracted a wave of remote workers and digital nomads, primarily from Northern Europe and the United States, whose purchasing power vastly exceeds that of local workers. In neighborhoods like Principe Real and Santos, it is not unusual for apartments to be listed at prices that assume a Northern European salary.

The displacement has been most acute in Lisbon's older, working-class neighborhoods. Residents who lived for decades in rent-controlled apartments found themselves pushed out when landlords took advantage of legal mechanisms to terminate old leases and renovate for the higher-end market. The elderly were disproportionately affected.

Maria do Carmo, 78, lived in the same apartment in Mouraria for forty-two years before being evicted in 2024 after her landlord obtained a renovation permit. She now lives with her daughter's family in Amadora, a suburban municipality. "Mouraria is my home," she said. "I was baptized there. My husband died there. Now I am a visitor."

Protest movements have grown in scale and organization. The housing rights group Casa Para Todos organized demonstrations that drew tens of thousands of participants in 2023 and 2024. Squatting actions, once rare in Lisbon, have increased. A network of housing cooperatives is attempting to develop alternative ownership models, though they face significant bureaucratic and financial obstacles.

The Lisbon municipal government has committed to building 6,000 affordable housing units by 2028, a target that housing advocates describe as insufficient given the scale of need. Waiting lists for public housing in Lisbon currently exceed 30,000 applications.

Portugal's broader economic trajectory complicates the picture. Tourism accounts for roughly fifteen percent of GDP, and Lisbon is the country's primary tourist destination. Policies that restrict tourism-related real estate activity risk undermining a sector that the economy depends on. The tension between economic growth and housing affordability is real and unresolved.

Ana Sousa has begun attending Casa Para Todos meetings. She said she does not expect immediate change but feels the need to participate. "If the teachers and nurses and firefighters cannot live here," she said, "then who is Lisbon for?"
