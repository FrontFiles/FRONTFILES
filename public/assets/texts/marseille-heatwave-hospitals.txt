Breaking Point: Marseille Hospitals Overwhelmed by February Heat

By Sophie Delvaux
Marseille, France -- February 2026

Dr. Isabelle Faure had not slept in thirty-one hours when she sat down in the break room of Hopital de la Timone and tried to explain what was happening to her hospital. She is the head of the emergency department, and for the past eleven days she had been managing a crisis that no French hospital was designed to handle: a February heatwave.

Temperatures in Marseille reached 34 degrees Celsius on February 14th, shattering the previous record for the month by nearly eight degrees. They stayed above 30 for nine consecutive days. In a city where most residential buildings lack air conditioning and where the elderly population is among the largest in France, the consequences were immediate and severe.

"We started seeing heatstroke cases on the second day," Faure said. "By the fourth day, we had exhausted our ICU capacity. By the sixth day, we were diverting ambulances to hospitals in Aix and Aubagne. In February. This is not supposed to happen in February."

The Marseille hospital system, which serves a metropolitan area of approximately 1.9 million people, operates with roughly 870 ICU beds across its public and private facilities. During the heatwave, ICU occupancy reached 114 percent, with overflow patients housed in post-operative recovery rooms and repurposed conference spaces. Ventilators borrowed from a national stockpile arrived on the fifth day. They were needed immediately.

The victims were overwhelmingly elderly. Of the 347 heat-related hospital admissions recorded during the event, 281 were patients over the age of seventy. Of those, 64 died. The youngest was sixty-seven. The oldest was ninety-four.

France has painful experience with heat mortality. The 2003 heatwave killed approximately 15,000 people nationwide, a catastrophe that led to the creation of the Plan National Canicule -- a comprehensive heat action plan that includes public cooling centers, welfare checks on isolated elderly residents, and hospital surge protocols. The system has been credited with significantly reducing deaths in subsequent heatwaves, including the severe events of 2019 and 2022.

But the Plan Canicule is designed for summer. Its activation triggers are calibrated for June through September. When temperatures spiked in February, the institutional response was slower than it would have been in July. Cooling centers were not yet staffed. Municipal welfare check programs had not been activated for the season. Some nursing homes had not yet serviced their cooling equipment.

"The plan works," said Dr. Antoine Riviere, a public health researcher at Aix-Marseille University who studies heat mortality. "But it works for a climate that no longer exists. We are now seeing heat events outside the historical window, and our response infrastructure has not adapted."

The hospital staff bore the weight of that gap. Nurses in the Timone emergency department worked twelve-hour shifts that routinely extended to sixteen. A physician's assistant described treating patients in a hallway because every examination room was occupied. Cafeteria workers brought water to waiting areas where families sat for hours. The hospital chaplain was called more frequently than usual.

Burnout among healthcare workers in Marseille was already severe before the heatwave. A 2025 survey by the French Hospital Federation found that forty-three percent of nurses in the Bouches-du-Rhone department reported symptoms of clinical burnout, and twenty-seven percent said they were actively considering leaving the profession. The February crisis intensified those pressures.

"You cannot ask people to give what they do not have," said Marie-Claire Bonnard, a nurse union representative at Timone. "We gave everything during COVID. We gave everything during the summer heatwaves. And now February. There is no reserve left."

The Marseille municipal government announced emergency funding for expanded cooling infrastructure and committed to activating heat response protocols year-round beginning in 2027. The national health ministry dispatched an assessment team that spent four days evaluating the hospital system's capacity. Their report, expected in April, will inform budget discussions for the next fiscal year.

In the meantime, climate scientists warned that the February event, while extraordinary by historical standards, is consistent with projections for the western Mediterranean basin under current warming trajectories. Dr. Riviere noted that Marseille should expect at least one off-season heat event per year within the next decade.

On the day the heatwave finally broke, a mistral wind swept down from the north and dropped temperatures by fifteen degrees in six hours. The emergency department at Timone saw its admission rate fall by half overnight. Dr. Faure went home and slept for twelve hours.

When she returned the next morning, three more patients from the heatwave had died overnight. The crisis was over. The consequences were not.
