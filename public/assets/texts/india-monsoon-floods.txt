Water Without End: Dispatches From Assam's Monsoon Catastrophe

By Priya Sharma
BARPETA, Assam, India --

The water arrived at Fatima Begum's house at three in the morning. She was asleep on a raised bamboo platform with her two daughters and her mother-in-law when the sound woke her. Not a crash or a roar, but a slow, persistent gurgling, as if the earth itself were swallowing something. By the time she lit the kerosene lamp, the floor of her mud-brick home was covered in brown water that smelled of sewage and diesel fuel.

Within two hours, the water reached her waist. She carried her youngest daughter on her shoulders and walked through the dark toward higher ground, following a road she could no longer see. Her mother-in-law held onto the back of her sari. Her older daughter, who is eleven, waded alongside them. They reached a government school building on a small hill just before dawn. It was already crowded with more than two hundred people.

This is the monsoon in Assam, a state in northeastern India where catastrophic flooding has become an annual event so predictable that relief agencies begin pre-positioning supplies in April. But the scale of this year's disaster has exceeded even the worst projections. According to the Assam State Disaster Management Authority, more than five million people across twenty-eight of the state's thirty-five districts have been affected. Over three hundred thousand have been displaced from their homes. The official death toll stands at ninety-four, though local officials acknowledge that the number is almost certainly incomplete because many remote communities remain unreachable.

The Brahmaputra River, which flows through the center of the state, has been running above the danger mark at multiple gauging stations for more than three consecutive weeks, the longest such period in recorded history. Tributaries feeding into the Brahmaputra from the hills of Meghalaya and Arunachal Pradesh have also overflowed, creating a network of inundation that has turned vast stretches of the Barpeta and Nalbari districts into shallow lakes.

Rescue operations are being conducted by a mix of the National Disaster Response Force, the Indian Army, and volunteer groups using country boats and inflatable rafts. Colonel Deepak Rawat, who commands a rescue detachment operating out of a forward base near Barpeta town, says his teams are running sorties from first light until visibility drops in the evening. They are prioritizing pregnant women, elderly residents, and people with chronic illnesses who need regular medication.

"The challenge is not just reaching people," Colonel Rawat says. "It is convincing them to leave. Many families refuse to evacuate because they do not want to abandon their livestock. A family's cattle may be worth more than their house. If the animals drown, the family loses its income for the next year."

Infrastructure damage has been severe. The state highway connecting Barpeta to Guwahati, the capital, has been submerged at multiple points, cutting off road access and forcing relief supplies to be transported by boat. At least forty bridges across the state have been damaged or destroyed, according to the Public Works Department. Cell phone towers have lost power in several districts, leaving communities unable to call for help.

In the relief camps, conditions are strained. The school building where Fatima Begum took shelter was designed to hold three hundred students. It now houses nearly eight hundred displaced residents. Cooking is done in the courtyard using firewood scavenged from floating debris. The single functioning toilet serves the entire population. A team from Doctors Without Borders arrived three days after the flooding began and has been treating cases of diarrheal disease, skin infections, and respiratory illness, conditions that flourish in crowded, unsanitary environments.

The state government has announced a relief package that includes rice, lentils, and a cash payment of roughly thirty-five dollars per affected family. For many residents, the amount is so small relative to their losses that it registers as more of an insult than a lifeline. Agricultural damage alone is expected to exceed five hundred million dollars, with rice paddies, tea gardens, and vegetable plots destroyed across the affected districts.

Climate scientists have linked the increasing severity of Assam's monsoon flooding to a combination of factors, including more intense rainfall events driven by rising global temperatures, deforestation in upstream watersheds that accelerates runoff, and the encroachment of human settlements onto natural floodplains. The Brahmaputra basin is warming faster than the global average, and hydrological models suggest that extreme flood events in the region will become both more frequent and more intense over the coming decades.

Fatima Begum does not speak in terms of climate models. She speaks in terms of water marks on her walls. The floods came in 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023. Each year the mark was higher. Each year the recovery took longer. She does not know where she will live when the water recedes this time. She knows only that it will recede, and that it will return.
