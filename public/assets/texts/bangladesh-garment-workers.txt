Stitched Together: Inside the Lives of Dhaka's Garment Workers

By Nusrat Amin
DHAKA, Bangladesh --

The alarm on Rehana Akhter's phone goes off at five in the morning, and by five-fifteen she is walking through the narrow lanes of Mirpur, a densely packed neighborhood in northwest Dhaka where tens of thousands of garment workers live in subdivided apartments and shared rooms. The walk to her factory takes twenty-five minutes. She has made it more than three thousand times.

Rehana is thirty-one years old. She has worked in Bangladesh's ready-made garment industry since she was seventeen, moving through a series of factories that produce shirts, trousers, and jackets for brands whose names she recognizes from the labels she sews but whose stores she has never visited. She currently earns twelve thousand five hundred taka per month, roughly one hundred and fifteen dollars, as a senior sewing machine operator at a factory that employs about two thousand workers and ships primarily to European retailers.

Her wage is above the national minimum for the garment sector, which was raised in late 2023 after months of worker protests that shut down hundreds of factories and drew international attention. The new minimum of twelve thousand five hundred taka represented a fifty-six percent increase from the previous floor but still fell short of the twenty-three thousand taka that labor unions had demanded, a figure they calculated as the minimum necessary to cover food, housing, transportation, and basic healthcare for a single worker in Dhaka.

Bangladesh's garment industry is the second largest in the world after China's, generating more than forty-five billion dollars in annual export revenue and employing roughly four million workers, approximately eighty percent of them women. The sector accounts for more than eighty percent of the country's total export earnings and has been the primary engine of Bangladesh's economic growth over the past three decades. The brands that source from Bangladeshi factories include many of the world's largest fashion retailers, drawn by production costs that remain among the lowest in the global apparel supply chain.

The industry's safety record has been defined by a single catastrophic event. On April 24, 2013, the Rana Plaza building in the Dhaka suburb of Savar collapsed, killing one thousand one hundred and thirty-four garment workers and injuring more than two thousand five hundred others. The building had been constructed with substandard materials on swampy ground, and cracks had been reported in its structure the day before the collapse. Workers were ordered to return to their sewing machines despite the warnings. The disaster was the deadliest structural failure in the history of the modern garment industry and provoked a global reckoning over the conditions under which cheap clothing is produced.

In the years since Rana Plaza, safety conditions in Bangladeshi garment factories have improved measurably, though unevenly. The Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety, a legally binding agreement between international brands and trade unions, conducted inspections of more than sixteen hundred factories and mandated structural, electrical, and fire safety renovations. Factories that failed to meet the accord's standards were removed from the supply chains of signatory brands. The accord has been credited with significantly reducing the risk of another large-scale structural collapse.

But the improvements have limits. The accord covers only factories that supply signatory brands. An estimated several thousand smaller factories, many of which operate as subcontractors in the lower tiers of the supply chain, were never inspected under the agreement and remain outside its enforcement mechanism. Working conditions in these facilities are widely reported to be worse than in the larger, internationally audited factories. Reports of locked fire exits, inadequate ventilation, and excessive overtime persist.

Union organizing remains difficult and in many cases dangerous. Bangladeshi labor law technically protects the right to form unions, but in practice workers who attempt to organize face dismissal, blacklisting, and physical intimidation. A 2023 report by the Worker Rights Consortium documented cases in which union leaders were beaten by factory management's hired enforcers and subsequently fired without recourse. The government's labor inspectorate, responsible for enforcing workplace standards, employs fewer than one thousand inspectors to cover tens of thousands of registered factories.

Rehana Akhter is not a union member. She says she considered joining one several years ago but decided against it after a colleague who had been active in organizing efforts was dismissed and unable to find work at any factory in the area for more than a year.

"Everyone knows what happens," Rehana says, guiding fabric through her sewing machine with a practiced motion that does not slow as she speaks. "You speak up, you lose your job. You lose your job, your family does not eat. It is a simple calculation."

Her factory is one of the better ones. It was renovated under the accord, with new fire escapes, sprinkler systems, and structural reinforcements. The production floor is reasonably ventilated, and the lighting is adequate. Management provides a subsidized lunch of rice and dal in a ground-floor canteen. These conditions, which would be considered baseline in a European or North American factory, are regarded as above average in Dhaka.

The garment industry's future in Bangladesh is tied to questions that extend beyond the factory floor. Rising wages, while still low by global standards, are gradually eroding the cost advantage that made Bangladesh the destination of choice for price-sensitive brands. Some production is shifting to countries with even lower labor costs, including Ethiopia and Myanmar. Automation technologies threaten to reduce the demand for the manual sewing work that employs millions of Bangladeshi women.

Rehana finishes her shift at six in the evening and walks home through streets that are louder and more crowded than they were at dawn. She stops at a market stall to buy vegetables for dinner. She will eat, sleep, and return to the factory in the morning. The shirt she spent the day sewing will eventually hang in a store in Hamburg or Manchester, priced at sixty or seventy dollars. Her contribution to its existence is measured in minutes and fractions of a cent.

She does not think about the shirt. She thinks about her daughter, who is nine and attends a school that Rehana's wages make possible. The girl, Rehana says, is good at mathematics. She will not work in a factory.
