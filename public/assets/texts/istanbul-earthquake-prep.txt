The Fault Beneath the City: Istanbul's Unfinished Reckoning with Earthquake Risk

By Kemal Aydin
Istanbul, Turkey -- October 2025

Seismologists no longer speak of whether a major earthquake will strike Istanbul. They speak of when. The North Anatolian Fault, one of the most active tectonic boundaries on Earth, runs directly beneath the Sea of Marmara, less than twenty kilometers south of the city center. Over the past century, a sequence of large earthquakes has migrated progressively westward along the fault, each one closer to Istanbul. The 1999 Izmit earthquake, which killed more than 17,000 people 100 kilometers to the east, is widely interpreted as the penultimate event in this sequence.

The next rupture, according to consensus estimates from Turkish and international seismologists, is expected to produce a magnitude 7.0 to 7.5 earthquake. The probability of this event occurring before 2040 is assessed at roughly seventy percent. In a city of sixteen million people, a significant portion of whom live and work in buildings that cannot withstand such shaking, the consequences are projected to be catastrophic.

"We have known this for twenty-five years," said Professor Celal Sengor, a geologist at Istanbul Technical University. "The geology is not ambiguous. The question is what we have done with what we know."

The answer, measured against the scale of the threat, is both substantial and insufficient. Turkey strengthened its building codes after the 1999 earthquake, and new construction in Istanbul is generally built to modern seismic standards. The government launched an urban transformation program in 2012 aimed at demolishing and rebuilding the most vulnerable structures. According to official figures, more than 500,000 housing units in Istanbul have been reconstructed or reinforced under the program.

But Istanbul contains an estimated 1.2 million buildings that predate the 2000 building code, many of which were constructed during the city's rapid and largely unregulated expansion in the 1960s through 1990s. These structures -- mid-rise reinforced concrete apartment blocks, typically five to eight stories -- are the primary source of risk. Their concrete is often low-quality. Their rebar is insufficient. Their foundations sit on soft alluvial soil that amplifies seismic waves.

An independent engineering assessment conducted in 2024 by a consortium of Turkish universities estimated that approximately 90,000 buildings in Istanbul are at high risk of collapse or severe damage in a magnitude 7.2 earthquake. The study, which was not officially endorsed by the government, projected between 30,000 and 50,000 fatalities, with up to 200,000 serious injuries and more than one million people displaced.

Building code violations in new construction remain a persistent concern. Municipal inspection records obtained through legal requests show that enforcement is uneven across Istanbul's 39 districts. In some peripheral districts where construction is booming, the ratio of buildings to inspectors exceeds 5,000 to one. Engineers who have worked as inspectors describe a system where political pressure, corruption, and understaffing combine to allow substandard construction to proceed.

"I have personally signed off on buildings I knew were problematic," said one former municipal inspector who left the profession. "When you report violations, you are told to reconsider. When you persist, you are reassigned. I left because I could not sleep."

Evacuation planning presents its own challenges. Istanbul's geography -- bisected by the Bosphorus, ringed by water on three sides, and connected internally by a road network that is chronically congested -- makes rapid evacuation of affected areas extremely difficult. The city's emergency management authority, AFAD, has designated assembly points in parks and open spaces throughout the city, but critics note that many of these areas are surrounded by the same vulnerable buildings that would collapse in a major earthquake.

AFAD officials defend their preparations, citing investments in emergency supply stockpiles, search and rescue training, hospital surge capacity planning, and public awareness campaigns. "Istanbul is the most earthquake-prepared city in Turkey," said a senior AFAD coordinator. "That does not mean we are fully prepared. No city of this size and complexity can be fully prepared."

The February 2023 earthquakes in southeastern Turkey, which killed more than 50,000 people, injected new urgency into the Istanbul discussion. The Kahramanmaras disaster demonstrated what happens when building code enforcement fails at scale, and it exposed weaknesses in the national emergency response system that have direct implications for Istanbul.

In the neighborhoods of Fatih and Beyoglu, in the dense residential streets of Bagcilar and Esenyurt, millions of people continue their lives above a fault that is, by geological standards, overdue. They go to work. They send their children to school. They live in buildings whose structural integrity they cannot independently verify.

On a warm October afternoon, a small earthquake -- magnitude 3.1, centered in the Marmara -- rattled dishes in kitchens across the Asian side of the city. Social media lit up briefly. By evening, the conversation had moved on.

The fault did not.
