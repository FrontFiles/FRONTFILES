Waiting at the Edge: A Dispatch from Greece's Evros Border

By Elena Papadimitriou
Orestiada, Evros Prefecture, Greece -- December 2025

The temperature at the Evros River crossing dropped to minus seven degrees Celsius overnight, and the Greek border police found three people in a drainage culvert at dawn. A man, a woman, and a child, all from Afghanistan, wrapped in a single thermal blanket that was inadequate for the conditions. The child was hypothermic. A military medic administered first aid before the family was transported to a processing facility twelve kilometers south.

This is the Evros border in winter. It is a place where the European Union's migration policies meet the physical reality of a river, a climate, and the desperation of people who have run out of alternatives.

The Evros River, known as Meric in Turkish, forms a natural border between Greece and Turkey for roughly 180 kilometers. It has been a primary crossing point for irregular migration into the EU for more than two decades. In 2020, Greece constructed a forty-kilometer steel fence along sections of the border and deployed additional military and police personnel. The fortification reduced crossings significantly but did not eliminate them. People still come, finding gaps in the fence, crossing the river by raft, or wading through marshland in darkness.

The processing facility near Orestiada -- officially designated as a Reception and Identification Center -- was built to hold approximately 240 people. On the morning this reporter visited, it held more than 600. Men, women, and children from Afghanistan, Syria, Somalia, and Pakistan occupied every available space. Some had been there for weeks, waiting for asylum interviews that are scheduled sporadically and conducted through interpreters who are in short supply.

Conditions inside the facility are strained but not squalid. The Greek government, with EU funding, has invested in basic infrastructure -- heated containers, portable toilets, a medical station staffed by the Hellenic Centre for Disease Control. But the overcrowding has pushed the facility well past its design capacity, and the waiting is corrosive. Fights break out periodically. Mental health cases are rising.

"The system was not built for this volume," said a facility administrator who asked not to be identified. "We process people as fast as we can. But the law requires certain steps, and each step takes time, and there are not enough people to do the work."

Humanitarian organizations that once operated freely along the border have found their access increasingly restricted. Doctors Without Borders was denied permission to operate inside the Evros region in 2022 and has not been readmitted. The Greek Council for Refugees, a legal aid organization, maintains a small presence but reports difficulty arranging client visits. Several smaller NGOs have relocated to Thessaloniki or Athens, providing remote legal assistance through phone consultations.

The Greek government defends its border management as both lawful and necessary. Officials point to the security dimension of irregular migration, the strain on local communities, and the need to maintain order along an external EU border. They also note that Greece has processed hundreds of thousands of asylum applications since 2015 and that the country hosts a disproportionate share of the EU's migration burden relative to its size and resources.

These arguments carry weight. But they exist in tension with persistent allegations, documented by multiple international organizations, that Greek authorities have engaged in pushbacks -- the illegal practice of forcing migrants back across the border without allowing them to claim asylum. The European Court of Human Rights has heard multiple cases on the matter. Greek authorities deny the allegations.

At the border fence, surveillance cameras rotate on poles every few hundred meters. A Greek military patrol passes in an armored vehicle. On the Turkish side, the landscape is flat and featureless. Somewhere in between, the Evros flows dark and cold.

Ahmad, a 31-year-old civil engineer from Herat, Afghanistan, arrived at the processing facility nine days ago with his wife and two sons. He described a journey that took fourteen months and passed through Iran, Turkey, and a nighttime river crossing that nearly killed them. "The boat was plastic. Like a toy," he said. "The water came in. My son was screaming."

He said he was not angry, only tired. He wanted his asylum case decided so he could begin applying for work. He had heard the process could take months. He was prepared to wait.

Winter in Evros lasts well into March. The river freezes in patches but never fully. The crossings continue through the coldest months, because the people making them are driven by calculations of risk and hope that do not pause for weather.

At the medical station, a nurse wrapped a fresh blanket around the Afghan child brought in that morning. The child had stopped shivering, which was a good sign. Outside, it had begun to snow.
